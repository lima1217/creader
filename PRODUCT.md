# Product

## Register

product

## Users

CReader is a personal reading tool for its maker: a serious reader who keeps books, AI conversation, and durable reading notes local. The user is usually in a focused desktop reading session, moving between EPUB text, selected passages, contextual AI help, and a local Markdown Reading Memory.

## Product Purpose

CReader exists to make EPUB reading feel calm and locally owned while still letting AI assist with interpretation, questions, and source-grounded knowledge capture. Success means the app gets out of the way during reading, makes AI feel like a contextual companion rather than the center of the product, and preserves useful reading insights without turning every interaction into note-taking labor.

## Brand Personality

Quiet, literate, trustworthy. The interface should feel attentive and alive, but never loud. It should favor reading flow, patient hierarchy, and restrained desktop-native affordances over decorative novelty.

## Anti-references

CReader should not look or feel like a SaaS dashboard, a busy note-taking app, a chatbot-first AI product, a Kindle clone, or an Obsidian clone. Avoid interfaces that are dominated by persistent configuration, dense chrome, generic AI assistant framing, card-heavy productivity patterns, or over-branded reading surfaces.

## Design Principles

1. Reading is the center of gravity. Library, toolbar, AI, and memory features should support the current book without competing with it.
2. Configuration belongs away from the reading surface. Persistent panels should expose only what supports the current moment.
3. Use proximity instead of excess lines. Group related controls through spacing, alignment, shared motion, and local emphasis before adding borders or dividers.
4. Make interaction feel continuous. Nearby controls may subtly respond to pointer distance through scale, opacity, or tone, so the UI feels less binary than ordinary hover states.
5. Keep AI source-grounded and quiet. AI features should inherit book context, selection, progress, and CFI traces without making the product feel chatbot-first.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target clear, readable defaults with strong keyboard access, visible focus states, and reduced unnecessary visual separators. Motion should be subtle, stateful, and respectful of reduced-motion preferences. Proximity effects should enhance discoverability without being required to understand or operate the interface.
